Problem

How might a user interface be designed to positively reinforce healthy habits?

Some individuals struggle with maintaining a clean living space, oftentimes as a byproduct of declining mental health and eventually leading to strained relationships with others.

Brief

Messiness is more than just a personality trait. In many cases it’s simply a habit that results from procrastination, but it may manifest in someone underprepared to maintain their own living space for the first time as well. For individuals struggling with chronic illness or mental health in particular, keeping up with household tasks can also pose a significant challenge.

Challenge

Identifying solutions to organize recurring tasks and mobilize time management skills in order to foster a cleaner living environment

User Research

Current undergraduate and graduate students (ages 18 to 26):

  • How did you learn basic household skills?
  • How would you describe your cleaning habits?
  • Do you consider the general upkeep of your living space a priority?
  • What do you like or dislike about the current upkeep of your living space?
  • How is your experience keeping a cleaning routine living on your own vs living with roommates or family?
  • Have you ever struggled to keep your living environment clean as an adult?
  • When you have to do an unpleasant or boring task, how do you keep yourself motivated?

Pain Points

Lack of knowledge - some young adults are not taught basic household skills

Unclear boundaries - roommates have different expectations about cleanliness

Demotivation - students juggling multiple responsibilities procrastinate on household chores

Deprioritization - messy areas quickly accumulate when other tasks take priority

Goals

Positively reinforce healthy habits
Define and mobilize a realistic routine
Split responsibilities equitably with roommates or family members

User Personas

Alexis | 22 | Charleston
Outgoing, Warm, Easily distracted, Sociable

Alexis is a fourth year computer engineering major at Georgia Tech. She lived on campus for the first two years of school but then moved to an off-campus apartment for her last two years. She lives with one roommate and has a sorority meal plan, so she usually doesn’t cook for every meal.

Scenario

Since this is Alexis’s final undergraduate year, she’s busier and more stressed than ever. Alexis is finding it hard to balance her schoolwork, extracurricular activities, and post-graduation job search, and keeping on top of household chores always seems to slip her mind. She knows having a clean living space will be better for her physical and mental health, but when weekly messes pile up she feels overwhelmed with the amount of work she has to do and has a hard time getting started.

Patrick | 26 | Atlanta
Friendly, Easy-going, Athletic, Creative

Patrick is a graduate student studying mathematics at Georgia Tech. He lives alone in an off-campus apartment in Midtown. In his free time, he likes to run in Piedmont Park, attend local indie rock shows, and volunteer at the Atlanta Humane Society.

Scenario

Patrick has just proposed to his long-term girlfriend, and they plan to move in together sometime within the next few months. He has never shared a living space with a significant other before, and because his apartment is usually pretty messy he wants to change the way he lives so his fiancee isn’t put off by his habits or stuck doing all the cleaning herself. He has tried to be more organized in the past but ends up giving up within a few weeks. Patrick wants to be a helpful and considerate partner, but he’s having a difficult time keeping track of what needs to be done and when.

Matthew | 21 | Orlando
Responsible, Organized, Conscientious, Cooperative

Matthew is a third year industrial engineering major at Georgia Tech. He lives on campus in student apartments with three other roommates, all of whom are friends from his freshman year. He’s the oldest out of three siblings and is in the habit of cleaning up after himself and others as well.

Scenario

Matthew tries his best to be a good roommate by cleaning up after himself and being quiet when others are sleeping. However, he notices that his roommates aren’t cleaning the shared kitchen and bathroom on a regular basis. He’s tried to bring this up and his roommates just say they’ll catch up on the weekend, but they aren’t very consistent at doing so. Nobody in the apartment really has a set routine for dealing with housework, and Matthew doesn’t want to overstep any boundaries by telling people what to do.

Solution

The home screen shows an overview of household tasks: a daily progress panel, rooms with an urgency indicator, and a pomodoro-style timer feature. Users can tap the progress panel to view all scheduled tasks for the day, or they can tap into individual rooms to see those assigned tasks in particular.

Tapping into a particular room from the home screen shows the tasks assigned to the user for that room, as well as any other family members or roommates who may also have tasks due that day (if the room is a shared space). At the bottom is a brief preview of any tasks due for the next two days.

Tapping through to the options menu of a room brings up the complete task list for that room. Tasks are organized by how frequently they have to be done: daily, weekly, monthly, or seasonally. The app comes with tasks pre-loaded, but users can also edit or add additional tasks from this screen. Once a task is due, it appears in the user’s task list. Tasks for shared spaces rotate through each roommate or family member.

Users can add or remove family members or roommates from their group. Group members can be invited by email or QR code.

Activity is tracked in the History tab. Users can see long-term progress through a heat map of daily tasks completed. Streaks are counted for days without any overdue tasks.

Tapping on the blue timer button on the bottom panel starts a pomodoro-style timer, which counts 25 minutes of work followed by a 5 minute break and repeats until all tasks for the day are completed.
